			<div id="bodyContent" class="content"><div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><script>function mfTempOpenSection(id){var block=document.getElementById("mf-section-"+id);block.className+=" open-block";block.previousSibling.className+=" open-block";}</script><div class="mw-parser-output"><section class="mf-section-0" id="mf-section-0"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">This article is about the holding company originally named Federated Department Stores. For its department store chain, see <a href="/wiki/Macy%27s" title="Macy's">Macy's</a>.</div>

<p><b>Macy's, Inc.</b> (originally <b>Federated Department Stores, Inc.</b>) is an American <a href="/wiki/Holding_company" title="Holding company">holding company</a> founded by Xavier Warren in 1929. Upon its establishment, Federated held ownership of the regional <a href="/wiki/Department_store" title="Department store">department store</a> chains <a href="/wiki/Abraham_%26_Straus" title="Abraham &amp; Straus">Abraham &amp; Straus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lazarus_(department_store)" title="Lazarus (department store)">Lazarus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Filene%27s" title="Filene's">Filene's</a>, and <a href="/wiki/John_Shillito_Company" title="John Shillito Company">Shillito's</a>. <a href="/wiki/Bloomingdale%27s" title="Bloomingdale's">Bloomingdale's</a> joined Federated Department Stores the following year. Throughout its early history, frequent acquisitions and divestitures saw the company operate a number of nameplates. In 1994, Federated took over the department store chain <a href="/wiki/Macy%27s" title="Macy's">Macy's</a>.  With the acquisition of <a href="/wiki/The_May_Department_Stores_Company" title="The May Department Stores Company">The May Department Stores Company</a> in 2005, the regional nameplates were retired and replaced by the Macy's and Bloomingdale's brands nationwide by 2006. Ultimately, Federated itself was renamed Macy's, Inc. in 2007.

It operates the subsidiaries Macy's, Bloomingdale's, and the beauty store chain <a href="/wiki/Bluemercury" title="Bluemercury">Bluemercury</a>, all of which have a <a href="/wiki/Flagship#Retailing" title="Flagship">flagship store</a> located in the <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> borough of <a href="/wiki/Manhattan" title="Manhattan">Manhattan</a>. As of November, 2019, the company operated 871 stores in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guam" title="Guam">Guam</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-storecount_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-storecount-2">[2]</a></sup> Its namesake locations and related operations account for 90% of its revenue. According to <a href="/wiki/Deloitte" title="Deloitte">Deloitte</a>, Macy's, Inc. is the world's largest fashion goods retailer and the 36th largest retailer overall, based on the company's reported 2010 retail sales revenue of $25 billion (equivalent to $29.3 billion in 2019).<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Macy%27s,_Inc.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-minerva-edit-enabled edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="1929.E2.80.932005:_Operations_as_Federated"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1929–2005:_Operations_as_Federated">1929–2005: Operations as Federated</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Macy%27s,_Inc.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 1929–2005: Operations as Federated" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-minerva-edit-enabled edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Federated Department Stores traces its corporate lineage to <a href="/wiki/Lazarus_(department_store)" title="Lazarus (department store)">F&amp;R Lazarus &amp; Company</a>, founded in Columbus, Ohio, in 1851. On November 25, 1929, Federated was founded by Xavier Warren in Columbus as a department store <a href="/wiki/Holding_company" title="Holding company">holding company</a> for Lazarus (including its Cincinnati division, then known as <a href="/wiki/Shillito%27s" class="mw-redirect" title="Shillito's">Shillito's</a>), <a href="/wiki/Abraham_%26_Straus" title="Abraham &amp; Straus">Abraham &amp; Straus</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Filene%27s" title="Filene's">William Filene's Sons</a> of Boston. In 1930, <a href="/wiki/Bloomingdale%27s" title="Bloomingdale's">Bloomingdale Brothers</a> of New York joined.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p><p>In the mid-1930s, a modern merchandising standard was set when <a href="/wiki/Fred_Lazarus_Jr." title="Fred Lazarus Jr.">Fred Lazarus Jr.</a> arranged garments in groups of a single size with a range of style, color and price, basing the technique upon observations made in Paris. As well, Lazarus convinced President <a href="/wiki/Franklin_D._Roosevelt" title="Franklin D. Roosevelt">Franklin D. Roosevelt</a> that it would help American economy to change the <a href="/wiki/Thanksgiving" title="Thanksgiving">Thanksgiving</a> holiday from the last Thursday of November to the fourth Thursday, thus extending the Christmas shopping season. An act of Congress perpetuated the arrangement in 1941. <a href="/wiki/Black_Friday_(shopping)" title="Black Friday (shopping)">Black Friday</a> became a nationwide sensation and the most profitable day for Federated. (Lazarus Jr. worked at Federated until he died in 2013, the last remaining family member with an official role at the company.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>)
</p><p>In 1945, Federated moved its corporate offices to <a href="/wiki/Cincinnati" title="Cincinnati">Cincinnati</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The latter half of the 20th century saw the company expand nationwide, adding <a href="/wiki/Rike_Kumler" class="mw-redirect" title="Rike Kumler">Rike Kumler</a> of Dayton, Ohio (merged into <a href="/wiki/Shillito%27s" class="mw-redirect" title="Shillito's">Shillito's</a> in the 1980s to become Shillito-Rike's); <a href="/wiki/Burdines" title="Burdines">Burdines</a> of Miami, Florida; <a href="/wiki/Rich%27s_(department_store)" title="Rich's (department store)">Rich's</a> of Atlanta, Georgia; <a href="/wiki/Foley%27s" title="Foley's">Foley's</a> of Houston, Texas; <a href="/wiki/Sanger_Brothers" class="mw-redirect" title="Sanger Brothers">Sanger Brothers</a> and A. Harris, both of Dallas, Texas (which was merged with Sanger Brothers to form <a href="/wiki/Sanger-Harris" class="mw-redirect" title="Sanger-Harris">Sanger-Harris</a>); <a href="/wiki/Boston_Store" title="Boston Store">Boston Store</a> of Milwaukee, Wisconsin; <a href="/wiki/MainStreet" title="MainStreet">MainStreet</a> of Chicago, Illinois; <a href="/wiki/Bullock%27s" title="Bullock's">Bullock's</a>, of Los Angeles; <a href="/wiki/I._Magnin" title="I. Magnin">I. Magnin</a>, of San Francisco, California; <a href="/wiki/Gold_Circle" title="Gold Circle">Gold Circle</a>; and <a href="/wiki/Richway" title="Richway">Richway</a> Discount Department Stores of Worthington, Ohio. Federated entered the supermarket industry in 1968 when it acquired the <a href="/wiki/Ralphs" title="Ralphs">Ralphs</a> chain based in Southern California.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> In 1982, Federated acquired the <a href="/wiki/Twin_Fair,_Inc." class="mw-redirect" title="Twin Fair, Inc.">Twin Fair, Inc.</a> discount store chain based in <a href="/wiki/Buffalo,_New_York" title="Buffalo, New York">Buffalo, New York</a>, and merged it with Gold Circle.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> In 1983, Federated sold four shopping center properties to <a href="/wiki/JMB_Realty" title="JMB Realty">JMB Realty</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p><p>Federated was taken over by <a href="/wiki/Robert_Campeau" title="Robert Campeau">Robert Campeau</a>, a Canadian real estate developer, in 1988.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Two years later, Federated filed for bankruptcy after Campeau failed to refinance the debt of Federated and Allied Stores Corp.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> In 1992, Campeau was ousted and Federated emerged from bankruptcy as a new public company. As part of the reorganization, Federated sold the Ralphs chain to a group of owners led by <a href="/wiki/Edward_J._DeBartolo_Corporation" class="mw-redirect" title="Edward J. DeBartolo Corporation">Edward J. DeBartolo Corporation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> That same year, Macy's declared bankruptcy; Federated acquired it two years later.  Federated began <a href="/wiki/E-commerce" title="E-commerce">selling goods online</a> in 1998, rather later than most contemporary large retailers; Federated ran a private bank, FDS Bank, which issued and maintained the majority of its own consumer credit card portfolio,<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> was one of the last credit card banks to begin to allow its cardholders to access account information online (around 2004).  The department store chain <a href="/wiki/Stern%27s" title="Stern's">Stern's</a>, a division of Federated, ceased operations in 2001 and most of its stores became Macy's stores.  In 2003, Federated changed the nameplates of almost all their non-Macy's stores — the lone exception was Bloomingdales — to include the Macy's name, a rebranding internally dubbed Project Hyphen. For example, <a href="/wiki/Seattle" title="Seattle">Seattle</a>-based <a href="/wiki/The_Bon_March%C3%A9" title="The Bon Marché">The Bon Marché</a> became Bon-Macy's; Goldsmith's in <a href="/wiki/Tennessee" title="Tennessee">Tennessee</a> became Goldsmith's-Macy's; Lazarus, Burdines, and Rich's also added "-Macy's" to their name. A year later, the hyphenated names were changed to simply Macy's, a rebranding process referred internally to as Project Star.
</p><p>In 1998, Federated settled an <a href="/wiki/U.S._Securities_and_Exchange_Commission" title="U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission">SEC</a> investigation for $14.46 million (equivalent to $22.7 million in 2020) due to unethical debt-collection practices. Federated routinely forced credit card holders/debtors to sign an agreement that legally bound them to repay their outstanding balances instead of having the unsecured debt discharge via the filing of <a href="/wiki/Bankruptcy" title="Bankruptcy">bankruptcy</a>. Federated failed to file reaffirmation agreements with bankruptcy courts. As a result, the changes in the agreements were not legally binding.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p><p>In 2001 Federated acquired Liberty House of Hawaii as it emerged from <a href="/wiki/Chapter_11,_Title_11,_United_States_Code" title="Chapter 11, Title 11, United States Code">Chapter 11 bankruptcy</a>. It was managed as part of Macy's West and all the store names were changed to Macy's.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template noprint Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability#Wikipedia_and_sources_that_mirror_or_use_it" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="This claim cites another Wikipedia article. Articles need references to reliable third-party sources. (October 2019)">circular reference</span></a></i>]</sup></p><p>In 2005, Federated agreed to sell its credit card business to <a href="/wiki/Citigroup" title="Citigroup">Citigroup</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p><p>In February, 2020, Macy’s announced that their headquarters in downtown Cincinnati will be closing in the near future with all corporate operations relocating to New York City. This news came after a gradual pulling out of the Cincinnati area with a couple of area store closures and the retirement of a top executive.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="2005.E2.80.932006:_Acquisition_of_May"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="2005–2006:_Acquisition_of_May">2005–2006: Acquisition of May</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Macy%27s,_Inc.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 2005–2006: Acquisition of May" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-minerva-edit-enabled edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Macy%27s_Inc.svg" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Macy%27s_Inc.svg/220px-Macy%27s_Inc.svg.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="95" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="381" data-file-height="165"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 220px;height: 95px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Macy%27s_Inc.svg/220px-Macy%27s_Inc.svg.png" data-alt="" data-width="220" data-height="95" data-srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Macy%27s_Inc.svg/330px-Macy%27s_Inc.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Macy%27s_Inc.svg/440px-Macy%27s_Inc.svg.png 2x" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Macy%27s_Inc.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Former Macy's, Inc logo, until 2019</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Federatedlogo.png" class="image"><noscript><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Federatedlogo.png/200px-Federatedlogo.png" decoding="async" width="200" height="50" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="3000" data-file-height="749"></noscript><span class="lazy-image-placeholder" style="width: 200px;height: 50px;" data-src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Federatedlogo.png/200px-Federatedlogo.png" data-alt="" data-width="200" data-height="50" data-class="thumbimage"> </span></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Federatedlogo.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The former Federated Department Stores logo, in use until 2007.</div></div></div>
<p>On July 18, 2005, <a href="/wiki/Federated_Department_Stores" class="mw-redirect" title="Federated Department Stores">Federated Department Stores</a> announced that it would acquire <a href="/wiki/May_Department_Stores" class="mw-redirect" title="May Department Stores">May Department Stores</a> company for $11 billion (equivalent to $14.4 billion in 2020) in cash and stock. Also part of the buyout was the bridal and formal unit of May, consisting of <a href="/wiki/David%27s_Bridal" title="David's Bridal">David's Bridal</a> and <a href="/wiki/After_Hours_Formalwear" class="mw-redirect" title="After Hours Formalwear">After Hours Formalwear</a>. Federated would also assume $6 billion (equivalent to $7.85 billion in 2020) of May's debt, bringing total consideration to $17 billion (equivalent to $22.3 billion in 2020). The deal would create the nation's largest department store chain with over 1,000 stores and $30 billion (equivalent to $39.3 billion in 2020) in annual sales. To help finance the deal, Federated agreed to sell its combined proprietary credit card business (but still administered by FACS Group, a subsidiary of Federated) to Citigroup. The merger was completed on August 30, 2005, after an assurance agreement was reached with the Attorneys General of <a href="/wiki/New_York_State" class="mw-redirect" title="New York State">New York</a>, <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maryland" title="Maryland">Maryland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a>.
</p><p>As a result of the merger, Federated also in the process reacquired two of their former department store chains Foley's &amp; Filene's (Which Federated originally sold to May Company), putting them back under the Federated Department Stores corporate umbrella for the first time since 1988.
</p><p>Federated announced plans to sell 80 store locations in 2006, having pledged in its settlement to sell most of them as viable businesses, with preference being given to a group of thirteen competitors. This number could fluctuate pursuant to Federated's negotiations with various mall landlords and its final decision regarding using former Macy locations for its luxury <a href="/wiki/Bloomingdale%27s" title="Bloomingdale's">Bloomingdale's</a> operation.
</p><p>On January 12, 2006, Federated announced its plans to divest May Company's <a href="/wiki/Lord_%26_Taylor" title="Lord &amp; Taylor">Lord &amp; Taylor</a> division (48 stores in 12 states) by the end of 2006 after utilizing prime and conflicting real estate by closing and converting several locations. On June 22, 2006, Macy's announced that <a href="/wiki/NRDC_Equity_Partners" title="NRDC Equity Partners">NRDC Equity Partners</a>, LLC would purchase <a href="/wiki/Lord_%26_Taylor" title="Lord &amp; Taylor">Lord &amp; Taylor</a> for US$1.2 billion (equivalent to $1.52 billion in 2020), and completed the sale in October 2006.
</p><p>On September 9, 2006, the former May Company store names <a href="/wiki/Famous-Barr" title="Famous-Barr">Famous-Barr</a>, <a href="/wiki/Filene%27s" title="Filene's">Filene's</a>, <a href="/wiki/Foley%27s" title="Foley's">Foley's</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kaufmann%27s" title="Kaufmann's">Kaufmann's</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hecht%27s" title="Hecht's">Hecht's</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Jones_Store" title="The Jones Store">The Jones Store</a>, <a href="/wiki/L._S._Ayres" title="L. S. Ayres">L. S. Ayres</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marshall_Field%27s" title="Marshall Field's">Marshall Field's</a>, <a href="/wiki/Meier_%26_Frank" title="Meier &amp; Frank">Meier &amp; Frank</a>, <a href="/wiki/Robinsons-May" title="Robinsons-May">Robinsons-May</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Strawbridge%27s" title="Strawbridge's">Strawbridge's</a> disappeared as Federated switched most of them to the <a href="/wiki/Macy%27s" title="Macy's">Macy's</a> masthead and a few to the Bloomingdale's name. The conversion of Marshall Field's in Chicago was particularly criticized, with many customers boycotting its historic State Street flagship store. The <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Tribune" title="Chicago Tribune">Chicago Tribune</a> continues to report on the poor reception of Macy's in Chicago. Pittsburgh customers also strongly resisted the name change from Kaufmann's, in part because of the <a href="/wiki/Edgar_J._Kaufmann" title="Edgar J. Kaufmann">Kaufmann family</a> role in Pittsburgh history, as well as the central store's Christmas windows and holiday parade.
</p><p>One of the consequences of this <a href="/wiki/Rebranding" title="Rebranding">rebranding</a> is that several malls have two Macy's stores. In downtown Boston, Federated liquidated an acquired Filene's because it already had a Macy's (formerly a Jordan Marsh) across the street. The two stores have a combined floorspace of more than 1,400,000 square feet (130,000 m<sup>2</sup>), more than two-thirds the size of Macy's New York City flagship store.
</p><p>On November 17, 2006, the bridal and formal unit was sold. David's Bridal and Priscilla of Boston were sold to <a href="/wiki/Leonard_Green_%26_Partners" title="Leonard Green &amp; Partners">Leonard Green &amp; Partners</a>. After Hours Formalwear was sold to <a href="/wiki/Men%27s_Wearhouse" class="mw-redirect" title="Men's Wearhouse">Men's Wearhouse</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span id="2007.E2.80.93present:_Operations_as_Macy.27s.2C_Inc."></span><span class="mw-headline" id="2007–present:_Operations_as_Macy's,_Inc.">2007–present: Operations as Macy's, Inc.</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Macy%27s,_Inc.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 2007–present: Operations as Macy's, Inc." data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-minerva-edit-enabled edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>On February 27, 2007, Federated announced that its board of directors would ask shareholders to change the company's name to <b>Macy's Group, Inc.</b> By March 28, the company revised its plans for the new name, opting to eventually become Macy's, Inc. Federated shareholders approved the revised proposal during the company's annual meeting on May 18, 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> The company was previously known as Federated Retail Holdings, Inc.
</p><p>The name took effect on June 1, 2007.  Reasoning for the proposed name change, according to <a href="/wiki/Terry_Lundgren" title="Terry Lundgren">Terry Lundgren</a>, Federated's chairman, president and chief executive officer — hinges on the large-scale conversions throughout the company toward the Macy's nameplate. "Today, we are a brand-driven company focused on Macy's and Bloomingdale's, not a federation of department stores," Lundgren said in the company's press release heralding the proposed name.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>  Upon the change to Macy's Inc., Federated's stock ticker symbol on the New York Stock Exchange changed from "FD" to "M", making the new Macy's Inc. one of a handful of single-letter <a href="/wiki/Ticker_symbol" title="Ticker symbol">ticker symbol</a> companies.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p><p>In April, 2008, Moody's Investors Service said that it may downgrade Macy's Inc. bonds to just above junk status.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> That same month, Fitch Ratings downgraded their ratings to BBB- from BBB, noting a deterioration in the company's operating and credit metrics. A rating of BBB- is one notch above junk status.
</p><p>The domain <i>macysinc.com</i> attracted at least 3 million visitors annually by 2008 according to a <a href="/wiki/Compete.com" title="Compete.com">Compete.com</a> survey.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p><p>On Wednesday, February 6, 2008, Terry Lundgren announced the localization strategy and the company's plan to shed 2,550 jobs.  This new localization strategy is known as "My Macy's."
</p><p>Employees of the Macy's North headquarters office in <a href="/wiki/Minneapolis" title="Minneapolis">Minneapolis</a>, the Macy's Northwest headquarters office in <a href="/wiki/Seattle" title="Seattle">Seattle</a>, and the Macy's Midwest headquarters office in <a href="/wiki/St._Louis" title="St. Louis">St. Louis</a> were given <a href="/wiki/Termination_of_employment#Pink_slip" title="Termination of employment">pink slips</a>, as Macy's pared its seven regional centers to four. About 40 new jobs were to be created in May as part of the restructuring. By 2009, the company expected to save $100 million (equivalent to $119 million in 2020) a year from the cuts.
</p><p>On February 2, 2009, Macy's announced the elimination of 7,000 jobs, or 4% of its work force, and slashed its dividend as it looked to lower expenses as part of major restructuring.
<a href="/wiki/Cincinnati" title="Cincinnati">Cincinnati</a>-based Macy's Inc. said the work force reduction includes positions in offices, stores and other locations. The cuts will include some unfilled jobs. 
"Reducing our workforce is an unfortunate outcome of the current economic environment, and I am frustrated that so many of our people will be unable to move forward with us as we proceed into a very exciting future for Macy’s and Bloomingdale's" said <a href="/wiki/Terry_J._Lundgren" class="mw-redirect" title="Terry J. Lundgren">Terry J. Lundgren</a>, chairman, president and chief executive officer. "
</p><p>Macy's also got rid of its division structure and integrated its functions into one organization. Macy's central buying, merchandise planning, stores senior management and marketing functions merged to its <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> corporate office (formerly <a href="/wiki/Macy%27s_East" title="Macy's East">Macy's East</a>). Corporate-related business functions, such as finance and human resources, will be primarily in Cincinnati. To buy with local consumers in mind, Macy's developed a concept called "My Macy's", in which the buyers and planners all look at what the local consumer base is looking for in their local Macy's store.  This will help bring a better sense of branding, sizing, and marketing to each Macy's store nationwide.
</p><p>Macy's Inc. decided to close the Bloomingdale's at the <a href="/wiki/Mall_of_America" title="Mall of America">Mall of America</a> in <a href="/wiki/Minnesota" title="Minnesota">Minnesota</a>. Since 1994, Bloomingdale's had been one of the 4 anchor stores of the mall, and will be replaced with a $30 million renovation with four new foreign clothing stores.
</p><p>On October 14, 2013, Macy's Inc. announced the decision to open most of their stores for the first time on <a href="/wiki/Thanksgiving_Day" class="mw-redirect" title="Thanksgiving Day">Thanksgiving Day</a> 2013, breaking a long-standing tradition of 155 years, and joined the ranks of retailers who created <a href="/wiki/Gray_Thursday" class="mw-redirect" title="Gray Thursday">Gray Thursday</a> the year before. Its doors opened at 8 p.m. (local time) on the holiday evening, and remained open for 24 hours straight until the close of business on Friday, which is usually about 10 p.m.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p><p>As of February 2014<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Macy%27s,_Inc.&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, Macy's Inc. is valued at US$28 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-Macy's_Sets_Strategy_to_Keep_Momentum_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Macy's_Sets_Strategy_to_Keep_Momentum-27">[27]</a></sup></p><p>In September 2015 Macy's announced it would close 40 stores, 5% of its total stores in early 2016. It also announced plans to open 6 additional <a href="/wiki/Macy%27s_Backstage" class="mw-redirect" title="Macy's Backstage">Macy's Backstage</a> locations. From 2010 to 2015, Macy's had closed 52 stores and opened 12.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>
