<p>The <b>Raytheon Company</b> is a major <a href="/wiki/List_of_United_States_defense_contractors" title="List of United States defense contractors">U.S. defense contractor</a> and industrial corporation with core <a href="/wiki/Manufacturing" title="Manufacturing">manufacturing</a> concentrations in <a href="/wiki/Weapon" title="Weapon">weapons</a> and military and commercial <a href="/wiki/Electronics" title="Electronics">electronics</a>. It was previously involved in corporate and special-mission <a href="/wiki/Aircraft" title="Aircraft">aircraft</a> until early 2007. class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10-K-2">[2]</a></sup></td></tr><tr><th scope="row" style="padding-right: 0.5em;">Website</th><td style="line-height: 1.35em;"><span class="url"><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://raytheon.com">raytheon<wbr></wbr>.com</a></span></td></tr></tbody></table><p>Established in 1922, the company <a href="/wiki/Incorporation_(business)" title="Incorporation (business)">reincorporated</a> in 1928 and adopted its present name in 1959. As of 2018, the company had around 67,000 employees worldwide and annual revenues of approximately <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">US$</a>25.35 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> More than 90% of Raytheon's revenues were obtained from military contracts and, as of 2012, it was the fifth-largest military contractor in the world.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> As of 2015<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Raytheon&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, it is the third largest <a href="/wiki/List_of_United_States_defense_contractors" title="List of United States defense contractors">defense contractor in the United States</a> by defense <a href="/wiki/Revenue" title="Revenue">revenue</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p><p>In 2003, Raytheon's headquarters moved from <a href="/wiki/Lexington,_Massachusetts" title="Lexington, Massachusetts">Lexington, Massachusetts</a>, to <a href="/wiki/Waltham,_Massachusetts" title="Waltham, Massachusetts">Waltham, Massachusetts</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The company had previously been headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Cambridge,_Massachusetts" title="Cambridge, Massachusetts">Cambridge, Massachusetts</a>, from 1922 to 1928, <a href="/wiki/Newton,_Massachusetts" title="Newton, Massachusetts">Newton, Massachusetts</a>, from 1928 to 1941, Waltham from 1941 to 1961 and Lexington from 1961 to 2003.
</p>

</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Raytheon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-minerva-edit-enabled edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">

<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_years">Early years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Raytheon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early years" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-minerva-edit-enabled edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 1922, two former <a href="/wiki/Tufts_University_School_of_Engineering" title="Tufts University School of Engineering">Tufts University School of Engineering</a>, roommates Laurence K. Marshall and <a href="/wiki/Vannevar_Bush" title="Vannevar Bush">Vannevar Bush</a>, along with scientist Charles G. Smith, founded the American Appliance Company in <a href="/wiki/Cambridge,_Massachusetts" title="Cambridge, Massachusetts">Cambridge, Massachusetts</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Its focus, which was originally on new <a href="/wiki/Refrigeration" title="Refrigeration">refrigeration</a> technology, soon shifted to <a href="/wiki/Electronics" title="Electronics">electronics</a>. The company's first product was a gaseous (<a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a>) <a href="/wiki/Rectifier" title="Rectifier">rectifier</a> that was based on Charles Smith's earlier <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomical</a> research of the star <a href="/wiki/Zeta_Puppis" title="Zeta Puppis">Zeta Puppis</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ordeal_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ordeal-10">[10]</a></sup> The electron tube was christened with the name <i>Raytheon</i> ("light of/from the gods"<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>) and was used in a <a href="/wiki/Battery_eliminator" title="Battery eliminator">battery eliminator</a>, a type of <a href="/wiki/Receiver_(radio)" class="mw-redirect" title="Receiver (radio)">radio-receiver</a> <a href="/wiki/Power_supply" title="Power supply">power supply</a> that plugged into the <a href="/wiki/Power_grid" class="mw-redirect" title="Power grid">power grid</a> in place of large <a href="/wiki/Battery_(electricity)" class="mw-redirect" title="Battery (electricity)">batteries</a>. This made it possible to convert household <a href="/wiki/Alternating_current" title="Alternating current">alternating current</a> to <a href="/wiki/Direct_current" title="Direct current">direct current</a> for radios and thus eliminate the need for expensive, short-lived batteries.
</p><p>In 1925, the company changed its name to Raytheon Manufacturing Company and began marketing its rectifier, under the Raytheon brand name, with commercial success. In 1928 Raytheon merged with Q.R.S. Company, an American manufacturer of electron tubes and switches, to form the successor of the same name, Raytheon Manufacturing Company.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>  By the 1930s, it had already grown to become one of the world's largest vacuum tube manufacturing companies.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In 1933 it diversified by acquiring Acme-Delta Company, a producer of <a href="/wiki/Transformer" title="Transformer">transformers</a>, power equipment, and electronic <a href="/wiki/Auto_parts" class="mw-redirect" title="Auto parts">auto parts</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="During_World_War_II">During World War II</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Raytheon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: During World War II" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-minerva-edit-enabled edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Early in <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, <a href="/wiki/Physicist" title="Physicist">physicists</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> invented the <a href="/wiki/Magnetron" class="mw-redirect" title="Magnetron">magnetron</a>, a specialized <a href="/wiki/Microwave" title="Microwave">microwave</a>-generating electron tube that markedly improved the capability of radar to detect enemy aircraft. American companies were then sought by the US government to perfect and <a href="/wiki/Mass-produce" class="mw-redirect" title="Mass-produce">mass-produce</a> the magnetron for ground-based, airborne, and shipborne radar systems, and, with support from the <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Institute_of_Technology" title="Massachusetts Institute of Technology">Massachusetts Institute of Technology</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Radiation_Laboratory" class="mw-redirect" title="Radiation Laboratory">Radiation Laboratory</a> (recently formed to investigate <a href="/wiki/Microwave_radar" class="mw-redirect" title="Microwave radar">microwave radar</a>), Raytheon received a contract to build the devices. Within a few months of being awarded the contract, Raytheon had already begun to mass manufacture <a href="/wiki/Magnetron" class="mw-redirect" title="Magnetron">magnetron</a> tubes for use in radar sets and then complete <a href="/wiki/Radar_system" class="mw-redirect" title="Radar system">radar systems</a>. At war's end in 1945 the company was responsible for about 80 percent of all magnetrons manufactured. During the war Raytheon also pioneered the production of shipboard radar systems, particularly for submarine detection. Raytheon ranked 71st among United States corporations in the value of World War II military production contracts.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p><p>Raytheon's research on the magnetron tube revealed the potential of microwaves to cook food. In 1945, Raytheon's <a href="/wiki/Percy_Spencer" title="Percy Spencer">Percy Spencer</a> invented the <a href="/wiki/Microwave_oven" title="Microwave oven">microwave oven</a> by discovering that the magnetron could rapidly heat food. In 1947, the company demonstrated the Radarange microwave oven for commercial use.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="After_World_War_II">After World War II</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Raytheon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: After World War II" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-minerva-edit-enabled edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 1945, the company expanded its electronics capability through acquisitions that included the Submarine Signal Company (founded in 1901), a leading manufacturer of maritime safety equipment. With its broadened capabilities, Raytheon developed the first <a href="/wiki/Guidance_system" title="Guidance system">guidance system</a> for a missile that could intercept a flying target. In 1948, Raytheon began to manufacture <a href="/wiki/Guided_missile" class="mw-redirect" title="Guided missile">guided missiles</a>. In 1950, its <a href="/wiki/SAM-N-2_Lark" title="SAM-N-2 Lark">Lark</a> missile became the first such weapon to destroy a target aircraft in flight. Raytheon then received military contracts to develop the air-to-air <a href="/wiki/AIM-7_Sparrow" title="AIM-7 Sparrow">Sparrow</a> and ground-to-air <a href="/wiki/MIM-23_Hawk" title="MIM-23 Hawk">Hawk</a> missiles, projects that received impetus from the <a href="/wiki/Korean_War" title="Korean War">Korean War</a>. In later decades, it remained a major producer of missiles, such as the <a href="/wiki/Patriot_antimissile_missile" class="mw-redirect" title="Patriot antimissile missile">Patriot antimissile missile</a> and the air-to-air <a href="/wiki/Phoenix_missile" class="mw-redirect" title="Phoenix missile">Phoenix missile</a>.
</p><p>Raytheon made a foray into computers, producing the <a href="/wiki/RAYDAC" title="RAYDAC">RAYDAC</a> computer for the U.S.Navy which became operational in 1953. "Unfortunately, the machine was technically obsolete by the time it was operational." Also in 1953 the company began work on a follow-on, the RAYCOM, which was never completed.<sup id="cite_ref-Flamm_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Flamm-13">[13]</a></sup> In 1954 it entered into a joint venture with <a href="/wiki/Honeywell" title="Honeywell">Honeywell</a> to form the Datamatic corporation. However it sold its interest to Honeywell a year later, before introduction of the <a href="/wiki/DATAmatic_1000" title="DATAmatic 1000">DATAmatic 1000</a> system.
</p><p>In 1959, Raytheon acquired the marine electronics company Apelco Applied Electronics, which significantly increased its strength in commercial marine navigation and radio gear, as well as less-expensive Japanese suppliers of products such as marine/weather band radios and <a href="/wiki/Radio_direction_finder" title="Radio direction finder">direction-finding gear</a>. In the same year, it changed its name to <b>Raytheon Company</b>.
</p><p>During the post-war years, Raytheon also made generally low- to medium-powered radio and television <a href="/wiki/Transmitter" title="Transmitter">transmitters</a> and related equipment for the commercial market, but the high-powered market was solidly in the hands of larger, better-financed competitors such as <a href="/wiki/Continental_Electronics" title="Continental Electronics">Continental Electronics</a>, <a href="/wiki/General_Electric" title="General Electric">General Electric</a> and <a href="/wiki/RCA" title="RCA">Radio Corporation of America</a>.
</p><p>In the 1950s, Raytheon began manufacturing <a href="/wiki/Transistor" title="Transistor">transistors</a>, including the <a href="/wiki/CK722" title="CK722">CK722</a>, priced and marketed to hobbyists.
</p><p>In 1961, the British electronics company <a href="/wiki/A.C._Cossor" title="A.C. Cossor">A.C. Cossor</a> merged with Raytheon, following its sale by <a href="/wiki/Philips" title="Philips">Philips</a>. The new Company's name was Raytheon Cossor. The Cossor side of the organisation is still current in the Raytheon group as of 2010<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Raytheon&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>.
</p><p>In 1965, it acquired <a href="/wiki/Amana_Refrigeration" class="mw-redirect" title="Amana Refrigeration">Amana Refrigeration</a>, Inc., a manufacturer of <a href="/wiki/Refrigerators" class="mw-redirect" title="Refrigerators">refrigerators</a> and <a href="/wiki/Air_conditioners" class="mw-redirect" title="Air conditioners">air conditioners</a>. Using the Amana brand name and its distribution channels, Raytheon began selling the first countertop household <a href="/wiki/Microwave_oven" title="Microwave oven">microwave oven</a> in 1967 and became a dominant manufacturer in the microwave oven business.
</p><p>In 1966, the company entered the educational <a href="/wiki/Publisher" class="mw-redirect" title="Publisher">publishing</a> business with the acquisition of <a href="/wiki/D.C._Heath_and_Company" class="mw-redirect" title="D.C. Heath and Company">D.C. Heath and Company</a>, marketing an influential physics textbook developed by the <a href="/wiki/Physical_Science_Study_Committee" title="Physical Science Study Committee">Physical Science Study Committee</a>. Raytheon also manufactured the <a href="/wiki/Apollo_Guidance_Computer" title="Apollo Guidance Computer">Apollo Guidance Computer</a>, which was introduced that year and flew aboard all <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> <a href="/wiki/Project_Apollo" class="mw-redirect" title="Project Apollo">Project Apollo</a> missions.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="1980s">1980s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Raytheon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 1980s" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-minerva-edit-enabled edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 1980, Raytheon acquired <a href="/wiki/Beechcraft" title="Beechcraft">Beech Aircraft Corporation</a>, a leading manufacturer of general aviation aircraft founded in 1932 by <a href="/wiki/Walter_H._Beech" class="mw-redirect" title="Walter H. Beech">Walter H. Beech</a>. In 1993 the company expanded its aircraft activities by adding the Hawker line of business jets by acquiring Corporate Jets Inc., the business jet product line of <a href="/wiki/British_Aerospace" title="British Aerospace">British Aerospace</a> (now <a href="/wiki/BAE_Systems" title="BAE Systems">BAE Systems</a>). These two entities were merged in 1994 to become the Raytheon Aircraft Company. In the first quarter of 2007 Raytheon sold its aircraft operations, which subsequently operated as <a href="/wiki/Hawker_Beechcraft" title="Hawker Beechcraft">Hawker Beechcraft</a>, and since 2014 have been units of <a href="/wiki/Textron_Aviation" title="Textron Aviation">Textron Aviation</a>. The product line of Raytheon's aircraft subsidiary included <a href="/wiki/Business_jet" title="Business jet">business jets</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/Hawker_800XP" class="mw-redirect" title="Hawker 800XP">Hawker 800XP</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hawker_4000" title="Hawker 4000">Hawker 4000</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Hawker_400" title="Hawker 400">Beechjet 400A</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Beechcraft_Premier" class="mw-redirect" title="Beechcraft Premier">Premier I</a>; the popular <a href="/wiki/Beechcraft_Super_King_Air" title="Beechcraft Super King Air">King Air</a> series of twin turboprops; and piston-engine aircraft such as the <a href="/wiki/Beechcraft_Bonanza" title="Beechcraft Bonanza">Bonanza</a>. Its special-mission aircraft included the single-turboprop <a href="/wiki/T-6A_Texan_II" class="mw-redirect" title="T-6A Texan II">T-6A Texan II</a>, which the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force" title="United States Air Force">United States Air Force</a> and <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">United States Navy</a> had chosen as their primary <a href="/wiki/Training_aircraft" class="mw-redirect" title="Training aircraft">training aircraft</a>.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="1990s">1990s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Raytheon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 1990s" data-section="6" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-minerva-edit-enabled edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 1991, during the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_War" title="Gulf War">Persian Gulf War</a>, Raytheon's <a href="/wiki/Patriot_missile" class="mw-redirect" title="Patriot missile">Patriot missile</a> received great international exposure, resulting in a substantial increase in sales for the company outside the United States. In an effort to establish leadership in the defense electronics business, Raytheon purchased in quick succession Dallas-based <a href="/wiki/E-Systems" class="mw-redirect" title="E-Systems">E-Systems</a> (1995); <a href="/wiki/Chrysler_Corporation" class="mw-redirect" title="Chrysler Corporation">Chrysler Corporation</a>'s defense electronics and aircraft-modification businesses, which had previously acquired companies such as Electrospace systems (1996) (portions of these businesses were later sold to <a href="/wiki/L-3_Communications" class="mw-redirect" title="L-3 Communications">L-3 Communications</a>), and the defense unit of <a href="/wiki/Texas_Instruments" title="Texas Instruments">Texas Instruments</a>, <a href="/wiki/Texas_Instruments_DSEG" class="mw-redirect" title="Texas Instruments DSEG">Defense Systems &amp; Electronics Group</a> (1997). Also in 1997, Raytheon acquired the aerospace and defense business of <a href="/wiki/Hughes_Aircraft_Company" title="Hughes Aircraft Company">Hughes Aircraft Company</a> from <a href="/wiki/Hughes_Electronics_Corporation" class="mw-redirect" title="Hughes Electronics Corporation">Hughes Electronics Corporation</a>, a subsidiary of <a href="/wiki/General_Motors" title="General Motors">General Motors</a>, which included a number of product lines previously purchased by Hughes Electronics, including the former <a href="/wiki/General_Dynamics" title="General Dynamics">General Dynamics</a> <a href="/wiki/Missile" title="Missile">missile</a> business (Pomona facility), the defense portion of <a href="/wiki/Delco_Electronics" title="Delco Electronics">Delco Electronics</a> (Delco Systems Operations), and <a href="/wiki/Magnavox_Electronic_Systems" class="mw-redirect" title="Magnavox Electronic Systems">Magnavox Electronic Systems</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p><p>Raytheon also divested itself of several nondefense businesses in the 1990s, including <a href="/wiki/Amana_Refrigeration" class="mw-redirect" title="Amana Refrigeration">Amana Refrigeration</a> and Seismograph Service Ltd (sold to <a href="/wiki/Schlumberger" title="Schlumberger">Schlumberger</a>-<a href="/wiki/Geco-Prakla" class="mw-redirect" title="Geco-Prakla">Geco-Prakla</a>). On October 12, 1999, Raytheon exited the <a href="/wiki/Personal_rapid_transit" title="Personal rapid transit">personal rapid transit</a> (PRT) business as it terminated its <a href="/wiki/PRT_2000" class="mw-redirect" title="PRT 2000">PRT 2000</a><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> system due to the high cost of development and the lack of interest.<sup id="cite_ref-Raytheon_PRT_Prospects_Dim_but_not_Doomed._Peter_Samuel._15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Raytheon_PRT_Prospects_Dim_but_not_Doomed._Peter_Samuel.-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="2000s">2000s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Raytheon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 2000s" data-section="7" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-minerva-edit-enabled edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In November 2007, Raytheon purchased <a href="/wiki/Sarcos" title="Sarcos">Sarcos</a> for an undisclosed sum, seeking to expand into robotics research and production.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p><p>In September 2009, Raytheon entered into an agreement to acquire <a href="/wiki/BBN_Technologies" title="BBN Technologies">BBN Technologies</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The acquisition was completed on October 29, 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="2010s">2010s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Raytheon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: 2010s" data-section="8" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-minerva-edit-enabled edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In December 2010, <a href="/wiki/Applied_Signal_Technology" title="Applied Signal Technology">Applied Signal Technology</a> agreed to be acquired by Raytheon for $490 million.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p><p>In October 2014, Raytheon beat rivals <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Martin" title="Lockheed Martin">Lockheed Martin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Northrop_Grumman" title="Northrop Grumman">Northrop Grumman</a> for a contract to build 3DELRR, a next-generation long-range radar system, for the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force" title="United States Air Force">US Air Force</a> worth an estimated $1 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p><p>The contract award was immediately protested by Raytheon's competitors, <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Martin" title="Lockheed Martin">Lockheed Martin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Northrop_Grumman" title="Northrop Grumman">Northrop Grumman</a>. After re-evaluating the bids following the protests,<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/US_Air_Force" class="mw-redirect" title="US Air Force">US Air Force</a>  decided to delay awarding the 3DELRR EMD contract until 2017 and was to issue an amended solicitation at the end of July 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> In 2017 the Air Force again awarded the contract to Raytheon.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p><p>In May 2015, Raytheon acquired cybersecurity firm Websense, Inc. from Vista Equity Partners for $1.9 billion<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> and combined it with RCP, formerly part of its IIS segment to form Raytheon|Websense.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> In October 2015, Raytheon|Websense acquired Foreground Security, a provider of security operations centers, managed security service solutions<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Use_plain_English#Buzzwords" title="Wikipedia:Use plain English"><span title="The material near this tag may use buzzwords designed solely to impress you or obscure meaning. (September 2019)">buzzword</span></a></i>]</sup> and cybersecurity professional services,<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> for $62 million.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> In January 2016, Raytheon|Websense acquired the firewall provider Stonesoft from <a href="/wiki/Intel" title="Intel">Intel</a> Security for an undisclosed amount and renamed itself to Forcepoint.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p><p>In July 2016, <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland's</a> Defence Minister <a href="/wiki/Antoni_Macierewicz" title="Antoni Macierewicz">Antoni Macierewicz</a> planned to sign a letter of intent with Raytheon for a $5.6 billion deal to upgrade its Patriot missile-defence shield.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p><p>In 2017, <a href="/wiki/2017_United_States%E2%80%93Saudi_Arabia_arms_deal" title="2017 United States–Saudi Arabia arms deal">Saudi Arabia signed business deals</a> worth billions of dollars with multiple American companies, including Raytheon.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p><p>In June 2019, Raytheon announced the intention to merge with <a href="/wiki/United_Technologies" title="United Technologies">United Technologies</a> to form the Raytheon Technologies Corporation. The combined company, valued at more than $100 billion after planned spinoffs, would be the world’s second-largest aerospace-and-defense company by sales behind Boeing.<sup id="cite_ref-Raytheon_merger_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Raytheon_merger-33">[33]</a></sup> Although UTC will be the nominal survivor, the merged company will be headquartered in Waltham rather than UTC's base in <a href="/wiki/Farmington,_Connecticut" title="Farmington, Connecticut">Farmington, Connecticut</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p><p>In July 2019, <a href="/wiki/Qatar" title="Qatar">Qatar</a>’s Ministry of Defense committed to acquire Raytheon’s <a href="/wiki/NASAMS" title="NASAMS">NASAM</a> and Patriot missile defense systems.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p><p>In February 2020, Raytheon completed the first radar antenna array for the US Army's new missile defense radar to replace the service's Patriot air and missile defense system sensor.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>

<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Raytheon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Overview" data-section="17" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-minerva-edit-enabled edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>Raytheon provides electronics, mission systems integration and other capabilities in the areas of sensing; effects; and command, control, communications and intelligence systems; as well as a broad range of mission support services. View an <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.raytheon.com/capabilities/products/index.html">alphabetical listing</a> of Raytheon's major products and services. See <i><a href="/wiki/Category:Raytheon_products" title="Category:Raytheon products">Raytheon products</a></i> for products manufactured and sold by Raytheon Company.
</p><p>Raytheon's electronics and defense-systems units produce air-, sea-, and land-launched <a href="/wiki/Missiles" class="mw-redirect" title="Missiles">missiles</a>, aircraft radar systems, weapons sights and targeting systems, communication and battle-management systems, and satellite components.

</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(5)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_record">Environmental record</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Raytheon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Environmental record" data-section="28" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-minerva-edit-enabled edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-5 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-5">
<p>Two lawsuits were filed against a Raytheon Company plant in St. Petersburg, Florida, due to concern with health risks, property values, and contamination in April 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-tampabay.com_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tampabay.com-51">[51]</a></sup> Raytheon was given until the end of the month to independently test whether or not the groundwater that originated from its area was contaminated. According to the <a href="/wiki/Florida_Department_of_Environmental_Protection" title="Florida Department of Environmental Protection">Florida Department of Environmental Protection</a> (DEP), the groundwater contained <a href="/wiki/Carcinogenic" class="mw-redirect" title="Carcinogenic">carcinogenic</a> contaminants, including <a href="/wiki/Trichloroethylene" title="Trichloroethylene">trichloroethylene</a>, <a href="/wiki/1,4-dioxane" class="mw-redirect" title="1,4-dioxane">1,4-dioxane</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Vinyl_chloride" title="Vinyl chloride">vinyl chloride</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> The DEP also reported that the clouds contained other toxins, such as lead and <a href="/wiki/Toluene" title="Toluene">toluene</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-tampabay.com_51-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tampabay.com-51">[51]</a></sup></p><p>In 1995, Raytheon acquired Dallas-based E-Systems, including a site in <a href="/wiki/St._Petersburg,_Florida" title="St. Petersburg, Florida">St. Petersburg, Florida</a>. In November 1991, prior to Raytheon's acquisition, contamination had been discovered at the E-Systems site. Soil and groundwater had been contaminated with the volatile organic compounds <a href="/wiki/Trichloroethylene" title="Trichloroethylene">trichloroethylene</a> and <a href="/wiki/1,4-Dioxane" title="1,4-Dioxane">1,4-Dioxane</a>. In 2005, groundwater monitoring indicated polluted groundwater was moving into areas outside the site.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> According to DEP documentation, Raytheon has tested wells on its site since 1996 but had not delivered a final report; therefore, it was given a deadline on May 31, 2008 to investigate its groundwater.<sup id="cite_ref-tampabay.com_51-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tampabay.com-51">[51]</a></sup> Contamination in the area has not affected anyone's drinking water supply or health, yet due to negative local media coverage lawsuits are being filed with claims against Raytheon citing decreases in property values.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p><p>In another case, Raytheon was ordered by the <a href="/wiki/Environmental_Protection_Agency" class="mw-redirect" title="Environmental Protection Agency">Environmental Protection Agency</a> (EPA) to treat groundwater at the Tucson Plant (acquired during the merger with Hughes) in Arizona since Raytheon used and disposed metals, <a href="/wiki/Chlorinated_solvent" class="mw-redirect" title="Chlorinated solvent">chlorinated solvents</a>, and other substances at the plant since 1951.<sup id="cite_ref-Environmental_Protection_Agency_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Environmental_Protection_Agency-55">[55]</a></sup> The EPA further required the installation and operation of an oxidation process system to treat the solvents and make the water safe to drink.<sup id="cite_ref-Environmental_Protection_Agency_55-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Environmental_Protection_Agency-55">[55]</a></sup></p><p>On 9 August 2006, The Stream Contact Centre in Derry, Northern Ireland, which had a contract with Raytheon at the time, was <a href="/wiki/Raytheon_9" title="Raytheon 9">attacked by protesters</a>. They destroyed the computers, documents, and mainframe of the office, and proceeded to occupy it for eight hours prior to their arrest.
</p><p>The activists were charged with criminal damage and affray. The trial of six of the accused began May 19, 2008, in the Laganside Courts in Belfast.
