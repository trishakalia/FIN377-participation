<p><b>United Technologies Corporation</b> (<b>UTC</b>) is an <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> <a href="/wiki/Multinational_corporation" title="Multinational corporation">multinational</a> <a href="/wiki/List_of_conglomerates" title="List of conglomerates">conglomerate</a> headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Farmington,_Connecticut" title="Farmington, Connecticut">Farmington, Connecticut</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Farmington_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Farmington-1">[1]</a></sup> It researches, develops, and manufactures products in numerous areas, including <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_engines" class="mw-redirect" title="Aircraft engines">aircraft engines</a>, aerospace systems, <a href="/wiki/HVAC" class="mw-redirect" title="HVAC">HVAC</a>, <a href="/wiki/Elevator" title="Elevator">elevators</a> and <a href="/wiki/Escalators" class="mw-redirect" title="Escalators">escalators</a>, fire and <a href="/wiki/Security" title="Security">security</a>, building automation, and industrial products, among others. UTC is also a large military contractor, getting about 10% of its revenue from the U.S. government.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Gregory_J._Hayes" title="Gregory J. Hayes">Gregory J. Hayes</a> is the CEO and chairman.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> On June 9, 2019, UTC announced the intent to merge its aerospace business with <a </section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_Technologies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-minerva-edit-enabled edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Pre-1970s">Pre-1970s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_Technologies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Pre-1970s" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-minerva-edit-enabled edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/United_Aircraft" title="United Aircraft">United Aircraft</a></div>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="1970s_and_1980s">1970s and 1980s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_Technologies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1970s and 1980s" data-section="3" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-minerva-edit-enabled edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 1974, <a href="/wiki/Harry_Gray_(business)" title="Harry Gray (business)">Harry Gray</a> left <a href="/wiki/Litton_Industries" title="Litton Industries">Litton Industries</a> to become the CEO of United Aircraft.<sup id="cite_ref-Fernandez1983_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fernandez1983-9">[9]</a></sup> He pursued a strategy of growth and diversification, changing the parent corporation's name to United Technologies Corporation (UTC) in 1975 to reflect the intent to diversify into numerous <a href="/wiki/High_tech" title="High tech">high tech</a> fields beyond aerospace.<sup id="cite_ref-Fernandez1983p246_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fernandez1983p246-10">[10]</a></sup> (The change became official on May 1, 1975.) The diversification was partially to balance civilian business against any overreliance on military business.<sup id="cite_ref-Fernandez1983_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fernandez1983-9">[9]</a></sup> UTC became a <a href="/wiki/Mergers_and_acquisitions" title="Mergers and acquisitions">mergers and acquisitions</a> (M&amp;A)–focused organization, with various forced takeovers of unwilling smaller corporations.<sup id="cite_ref-Fernandez1983_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fernandez1983-9">[9]</a></sup> The next year (1976), UTC forcibly acquired <a href="/wiki/Otis_Elevator" class="mw-redirect" title="Otis Elevator">Otis Elevator</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Fernandez1983pp246-251_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fernandez1983pp246-251-11">[11]</a></sup> In 1979, <a href="/wiki/Carrier_Corporation" title="Carrier Corporation">Carrier Refrigeration</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mostek" title="Mostek">Mostek</a> were acquired;<sup id="cite_ref-Fernandez1983pp260-264_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fernandez1983pp260-264-12">[12]</a></sup> the Carrier deal was forcible, while the Mostek deal was a <a href="/wiki/White_knight_(business)" title="White knight (business)">white knight</a> move against hostile takeover designs by <a href="/wiki/Gould_Electronics" title="Gould Electronics">Gould</a>.
</p><p>At one point the military portion of UTC's business, whose sensitivity to "excess profits" and boom/bust demand drove UTC to diversify away from it, actually carried the weight of losses incurred by the commercial M&amp;A side of the business.<sup id="cite_ref-Fernandez1983_9-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fernandez1983-9">[9]</a></sup> Although M&amp;A activity was not new to United Aircraft, the M&amp;A activity of the 1970s and 1980s was higher-stakes and arguably unfocused. Rather than aviation being the central theme of UTC businesses, <a href="/wiki/High_tech" title="High tech">high tech</a> (of any type) was the new theme. Some Wall Street watchers questioned the true value of M&amp;A at almost any price, seemingly for its own sake.<sup id="cite_ref-Fernandez1983_9-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fernandez1983-9">[9]</a></sup></p><p>Mostek was sold in 1985 to the French electronics company <a href="/wiki/Thomson_SA" class="mw-redirect" title="Thomson SA">Thomson</a>.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="1990s">1990s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_Technologies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 1990s" data-section="4" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-minerva-edit-enabled edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>UTC acquired <a href="/wiki/Sundstrand_Corporation" title="Sundstrand Corporation">Sundstrand Corporation</a> in 1999, and merged it into UTC's Hamilton Standard unit to form <a href="/wiki/Hamilton_Sundstrand" title="Hamilton Sundstrand">Hamilton Sundstrand</a>.
</p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="2000s">2000s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_Technologies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 2000s" data-section="5" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-minerva-edit-enabled edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In 2003, UTC entered the fire and security business by purchasing <a href="/wiki/Chubb_Security" title="Chubb Security">Chubb Security</a>.
</p><p>In 2004, UTC acquired the <a href="/wiki/Schweizer_Aircraft_Corporation" class="mw-redirect" title="Schweizer Aircraft Corporation">Schweizer Aircraft Corporation</a> which planned to operate as a wholly owned subsidiary under their <a href="/wiki/Sikorsky_Aircraft" title="Sikorsky Aircraft">Sikorsky Aircraft</a> division.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p><p>In 2005, UTC further pursued its stake in the fire and security business by purchasing <a href="/wiki/Kidde" title="Kidde">Kidde</a>. Also in 2005, UTC acquired Boeing's <a href="/wiki/Rocketdyne" title="Rocketdyne">Rocketdyne</a> division, which was merged into the <a href="/wiki/Pratt_%26_Whitney" title="Pratt &amp; Whitney">Pratt &amp; Whitney</a> business unit and renamed <a href="/wiki/Pratt_%26_Whitney_Rocketdyne" title="Pratt &amp; Whitney Rocketdyne">Pratt &amp; Whitney Rocketdyne</a> (later sold to <a href="/wiki/Aerojet" title="Aerojet">Aerojet</a> and merged into <a href="/wiki/Aerojet_Rocketdyne" title="Aerojet Rocketdyne">Aerojet Rocketdyne</a>.
</p><p>In 2007, UTC opened the Hawk Works, a Rapid Prototyping and Military Derivatives Completion Center (RPMDCC) located west of the <a href="/wiki/Elmira-Corning_Regional_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Elmira-Corning Regional Airport">Elmira-Corning Regional Airport</a> in <a href="/wiki/Big_Flats_(CDP),_New_York" title="Big Flats (CDP), New York">Big Flats, New York</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p><p>In March 2008, UTC made a $2.63 billion bid to acquire <a href="/wiki/Diebold" class="mw-redirect" title="Diebold">Diebold</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Canton,_Ohio" title="Canton, Ohio">Canton, Ohio</a> based manufacturer of banking and voting machines. Diebold rejected the  buyout bid as inadequate.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p><p>In November 2008, UTC's <a href="/wiki/Carrier_Corporation" title="Carrier Corporation">Carrier Corporation</a> acquired NORESCO, an energy service company.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p><p>In December 2009, it was announced that UTC would acquire a 49.5% stake in <a href="/wiki/Clipper_Windpower" title="Clipper Windpower">Clipper Windpower</a> for $206 million.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="2010s">2010s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_Technologies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 2010s" data-section="6" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-minerva-edit-enabled edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In April 2010, UTC announced that it was investing €15 million ($20 million) to set up the United Technologies Research Centre Ireland at <a href="/wiki/University_College_Cork" title="University College Cork">University College Cork</a>’s Tyndall National Institute which will carry out research on energy and security systems.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p><p>In October 2010, UTC agreed with Clipper to acquire the rest of the company.<sup id="cite_ref-utclip_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-utclip-22">[22]</a></sup></p><p>Also in 2010, UTC conducted its largest acquisition up to this year, <a href="/wiki/General_Electric" title="General Electric">General Electric</a>'s security equipment business for US$1.8 billion, a move to support <a href="/wiki/UTC_Climate,_Controls_%26_Security" title="UTC Climate, Controls &amp; Security">UTC's Fire &amp; Security unit</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-23">[23]</a></sup></p><p>In September 2011, UTC acquired a $18.4 billion deal (including $1.9 billion in net debt assumed) for aircraft components maker <a href="/wiki/Goodrich_Corporation" title="Goodrich Corporation">Goodrich Corporation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p><p>In June 2012, it was discovered that UTC sold military technology to the Chinese.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>  For pleading guilty to violating the <a href="/wiki/Arms_Export_Control_Act" title="Arms Export Control Act">Arms Export Control Act</a> and making false statements, United Technologies and its subsidiaries were fined $75 million.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p><p>In July 2012, United Technologies acquired <a href="/wiki/Goodrich_Corporation" title="Goodrich Corporation">Goodrich</a> and merged it with <a href="/wiki/Hamilton_Sundstrand" title="Hamilton Sundstrand">Hamilton Sundstrand</a>; the resulting organization is <a href="/wiki/UTC_Aerospace_Systems" class="mw-redirect" title="UTC Aerospace Systems">UTC Aerospace Systems</a>.
</p><p>In February 2013, <a href="/wiki/UTC_Power" title="UTC Power">UTC Power</a> was sold to <a href="/wiki/ClearEdge_Power" class="mw-redirect" title="ClearEdge Power">ClearEdge Power</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p><p>In October 2014, <a href="/wiki/Toshiba" title="Toshiba">Toshiba</a> and United Technologies made a deal to expand their joint venture outside <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p><p>In January 2015, UTC Building &amp; Industrial Systems completed the acquisition of <a href="/wiki/CIAT_Group" title="CIAT Group">CIAT Group</a>, a leading <a href="/wiki/HVAC" class="mw-redirect" title="HVAC">HVAC</a> manufacturing company in France.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> In November, <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Martin" title="Lockheed Martin">Lockheed Martin</a> completed its $9.0 billion acquisition of <a href="/wiki/Sikorsky_Aircraft" title="Sikorsky Aircraft">Sikorsky Aircraft</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p><p>In February 2016, UTC subsidiary <a href="/wiki/Carrier_Corporation" title="Carrier Corporation">Carrier Air Conditioner</a> announced to employees at its Indianapolis plant that Carrier is moving manufacturing to Mexico: “The best way to stay competitive and protect the business for long-term is to move production from our facility in Indianapolis to Monterrey, Mexico.” <sup id="cite_ref-Wall_Street_Journal:_Viral_Video_Over_Plant_Closure_Gets_Attention_in_GOP_Debate_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wall_Street_Journal:_Viral_Video_Over_Plant_Closure_Gets_Attention_in_GOP_Debate-31">[31]</a></sup> In December, Carrier agreed to keep the Indianapolis plant open, keeping 1,100 jobs in Indianapolis.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p><p>On September 4, 2017, UTC proposed to acquire <a href="/wiki/Rockwell_Collins" title="Rockwell Collins">Rockwell Collins</a> in cash and stock for $23 billion, $30 billion including Rockwell Collins' net debt, for $500+ million of synergies expected by year four.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p><p>On November 26, 2018, the company announced the Rockwell Collins deal had closed, and that it will split into three independent companies.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Pratt_and_Whitney" class="mw-redirect" title="Pratt and Whitney">Pratt and Whitney</a> and the newly-formed <a href="/wiki/Collins_Aerospace" title="Collins Aerospace">Collins Aerospace</a> will remain under United Technologies, while <a href="/wiki/Otis_Elevator" class="mw-redirect" title="Otis Elevator">Otis Elevator</a> and <a href="/wiki/UTC_Climate,_Controls_%26_Security" title="UTC Climate, Controls &amp; Security">UTC Climate, Controls &amp; Security</a> (doing business as <a href="/wiki/Carrier_Corporation" title="Carrier Corporation">Carrier</a>) will be spun off as two independent companies.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p><p>In June 2019, United Technologies announced the intention to merge with defense contractor <a href="/wiki/Raytheon" title="Raytheon">Raytheon</a> to form Raytheon Technologies Corporation. The combined company, valued at more than $100 billion after planned spinoffs, would be the world's second-largest aerospace-and-defense company by sales behind Boeing.<sup id="cite_ref-Raytheon_merger_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Raytheon_merger-7">[7]</a></sup> Although UTC will be the nominal survivor, the merged company will be headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Waltham,_Massachusetts" title="Waltham, Massachusetts">Waltham, Massachusetts</a>, where Raytheon is based.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
<h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Executive_history">Executive history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_Technologies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Executive history" data-section="7" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-minerva-edit-enabled edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>
<p>In April 2008, <a href="/wiki/Louis_R._Ch%C3%AAnevert" title="Louis R. Chênevert">Louis Chênevert</a> succeeded <a href="/wiki/George_David" title="George David">George David</a> as the company's <a href="/wiki/Chief_executive_officer" title="Chief executive officer">chief executive officer</a> (CEO).<sup id="cite_ref-:0_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-23">[23]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-nordqvist_2014_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nordqvist_2014-2">[2]</a></sup>  Chênevert served until 2014, when he was succeeded by <a href="/wiki/Gregory_J._Hayes" title="Gregory J. Hayes">Gregory Hayes</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-nordqvist_2014_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nordqvist_2014-2">[2]</a></sup></p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Chief_financial_officer" title="Chief financial officer">chief financial officer</a>'s (CFO) position was held by Gregory Hayes until 2014, when he succeeded Louis Chênevert as CEO.<sup id="cite_ref-nordqvist_2014_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nordqvist_2014-2">[2]</a></sup></p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Chairman_of_the_board_of_directors" class="mw-redirect" title="Chairman of the board of directors">chairman of the board of directors</a> (Chairperson) position went to Louis Chênevert, then the company's CEO, in January 2010, succeeding George David.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_23-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(2)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Finances">Finances</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_Technologies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Finances" data-section="8" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-minerva-edit-enabled edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-2 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-2">

<p>During the <a href="/wiki/United_States_elections,_2004" class="mw-redirect" title="United States elections, 2004">2004 election cycle</a>, UTC was the sixth largest defense industry donor to political campaigns, contributing a total of $789,561. Sixty-four percent of UTC's 2004 contributions went to Republicans. UTC was also the sixth largest donor to federal candidates and political parties in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_elections,_2006" class="mw-redirect" title="United States elections, 2006">2006 election cycle</a>. Thirty-five percent of those contributions went to Democrats; 53% of the funds were contributed to Republicans.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p><p>In 2005, United Technologies was among 53 entities that contributed the maximum of $250,000 to the second inauguration of President <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">George W. Bush</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(5)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Philanthropy">Philanthropy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_Technologies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Philanthropy" data-section="12" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-minerva-edit-enabled edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-5 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-5">
<p>In 1981, a contribution from UTC made possible the exhibition "Paris/Magnum: Photographs 1935–1981", featuring photographs of <a href="/wiki/Paris,_France" class="mw-redirect" title="Paris, France">Paris</a> taken by photographers of <a href="/wiki/Magnum_Photos" title="Magnum Photos">Magnum Photos</a>, the agency founded in 1947 by <a href="/wiki/Robert_Capa" title="Robert Capa">Robert Capa</a>, <a href="/wiki/George_Rodger" title="George Rodger">George Rodger</a>, <a href="/wiki/Henri_Cartier-Bresson" title="Henri Cartier-Bresson">Henri Cartier-Bresson</a>, <a href="/wiki/William_Vandivert" title="William Vandivert">William Vandivert</a>, and <a href="/wiki/David_Seymour_(photographer)" title="David Seymour (photographer)">David Seymour</a>.  A volume of the same title, with text by <a href="/wiki/Irwin_Shaw" title="Irwin Shaw">Irwin Shaw</a> and an introduction by <a href="/wiki/Inge_Morath" title="Inge Morath">Inge Morath</a>, was also published in 1981.
</p><p>UTC is the sponsor of the exhibition "Aphrodite and the Gods of Love" at <a href="/wiki/Boston%27s_Museum_of_Fine_Arts" class="mw-redirect" title="Boston's Museum of Fine Arts">Boston's Museum of Fine Arts</a> that opened in fall 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-MFA_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MFA-54">[54]</a></sup></p><p>UTC and its subsidiaries are major contributors to museums such as the <a href="/wiki/New_England_Air_Museum" title="New England Air Museum">New England Air Museum</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-New_England_Air_Museum_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-New_England_Air_Museum-56">[56]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(6)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_record">Environmental record</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_Technologies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Environmental record" data-section="13" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-minerva-edit-enabled edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-6 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-6">
<p>Researchers at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Massachusetts_Amherst" title="University of Massachusetts Amherst">University of Massachusetts Amherst</a> have identified UTC as the 38th-largest corporate producer of <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution_in_the_United_States" title="Air pollution in the United States">air pollution in the United States</a> as of 2008. UTC released roughly 110,000 pounds of toxic chemicals annually into the atmosphere<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> including <a href="/wiki/Manganese" title="Manganese">manganese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nickel" title="Nickel">nickel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chromium" title="Chromium">chromium</a> and related compounds.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup></p><p>In the 2016 <a href="/wiki/University_of_Massachusetts_Amherst" title="University of Massachusetts Amherst">University of Massachusetts Amherst</a> Toxic 100 Air Polluters Index, UTC was ranked 9th by a toxicity population exposure score. It was also reported they release 60,000 pounds of toxins into the air.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup></p>
