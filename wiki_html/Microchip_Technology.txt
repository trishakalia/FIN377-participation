Its products include <a href="/wiki/Microcontroller" title="Microcontroller">microcontrollers</a> (<a href="/wiki/PIC_microcontroller" class="mw-redirect" title="PIC microcontroller">PIC</a>, <a href="/wiki/PIC24#PIC24_and_dsPIC_16-bit_microcontrollers" class="mw-redirect" title="PIC24">dsPIC</a>, <a href="/wiki/Atmel_AVR" class="mw-redirect" title="Atmel AVR">AVR</a> and <a href="/wiki/Atmel_ARM-based_processors" title="Atmel ARM-based processors">SAM</a>), Serial <a href="/wiki/EEPROM" title="EEPROM">EEPROM</a> devices, Serial <a href="/wiki/Static_random_access_memory" class="mw-redirect" title="Static random access memory">SRAM</a> devices, embedded security devices, <a href="/wiki/Radio_frequency" title="Radio frequency">radio frequency</a> (RF) devices, thermal, power and battery management analog devices, as well as linear, interface and wireless solutions.
Examples of these solutions include <a href="/wiki/USB" title="USB">USB</a>, <a href="/wiki/ZigBee" class="mw-redirect" title="ZigBee">zigbee</a>, <a href="/wiki/MiWi" title="MiWi">MiWi</a>, <a href="/wiki/LoRa" title="LoRa">LoRa</a>, SIGFOX and <a href="/wiki/Ethernet" title="Ethernet">Ethernet</a>.

</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(1)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Microchip_Technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" data-section="1" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-minerva-edit-enabled edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-1 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-1">
<p>Microchip Technology was founded in 1987 when <a href="/wiki/General_Instrument" title="General Instrument">General Instrument</a> spun off its microelectronics division as a wholly owned subsidiary.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
Microchip Technology became an independent company in 1989 when it was acquired by a group of venture capitalists, and went public in 1993.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p><p>In April 2009, Microchip Technology announced the nanoWatt XLP Microcontrollers, claiming the world's lowest sleep current.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Microchip Technology had sold more than 6 billion microcontrollers as of 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p><p>In April 2010, Microchip acquired <a href="/wiki/Silicon_Storage_Technology" class="mw-redirect" title="Silicon Storage Technology">Silicon Storage Technology</a> (SST),<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
and sold several SST flash memory assets to <a href="/wiki/Greenliant_Systems" title="Greenliant Systems">Greenliant Systems</a> in May that year.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p><p>As of 2011, Microchip Technology ships over a billion processors every year. In September 2011, Microchip Technology shipped the 10 billionth PIC microcontroller.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p><p>In August 2012, Microchip acquired Standard Microsystems Corporation (SMSC).<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Among SMSC's assets were those it had previously acquired from <a href="/wiki/Symwave" class="mw-redirect" title="Symwave">Symwave</a>, a start-up that specialized in <a href="/wiki/USB_3.0" title="USB 3.0">USB 3.0</a> chips, and two hi-fi <a href="/wiki/Wireless_audio" class="mw-redirect" title="Wireless audio">wireless audio</a> companies — Kleer Semiconductor and Wireless Audio IP BV.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p><p>In January 2016, Microchip agreed to buy <a href="/wiki/Atmel" title="Atmel">Atmel</a> for $3.56 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/JPMorgan_Chase" title="JPMorgan Chase">JPMorgan Chase</a> advised Microchip while <a href="/wiki/Qatalyst_Partners" title="Qatalyst Partners">Qatalyst Partners</a> advised Atmel.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p><p>In March 2018, Microchip acquired <a href="/wiki/Microsemi_Corporation" class="mw-redirect" title="Microsemi Corporation">Microsemi Corporation</a> (NASDAQ: MSCC). The acquisition price represents a total equity value of about $8.35 billion, and a total enterprise value of about $10.15 billion, after accounting for Microsemi’s cash and investments, net of debt, on its balance sheet at December 31, 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
</section><h2 class="section-heading" onclick="javascript:mfTempOpenSection(2)"><div class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element indicator mw-ui-icon-small mw-ui-icon-flush-left"></div><span class="mw-headline" id="Products">Products</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Microchip_Technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Products" data-section="2" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-minerva-edit-enabled edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h2><section class="mf-section-2 collapsible-block" id="mf-section-2">
<p>Microchip develops a wide range of <a href="/wiki/Microcontroller" title="Microcontroller">microcontrollers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Integrated_circuit" title="Integrated circuit">integrated circuits</a> (ICs), for the hobbyist and professional markets.

<p><b>HI-TECH Software</b> was an Australian-based company that provides <a href="/wiki/ANSI_C" title="ANSI C">ANSI C</a> <a href="/wiki/Compiler" title="Compiler">compilers</a> and development tools. Founded in 1984, the company is best known for its HI-TECH C PRO compilers with whole-program compilation technology, or Omniscient Code Generation (OCG).<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> HI-TECH Software was bought by Microchip on 20 February 2009,<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> whereupon it refocused its development effort exclusively on supporting Microchip products.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p><p>Supported manufacturers and architectures :

<li><a href="/wiki/Cypress_Semiconductor" title="Cypress Semiconductor">Cypress</a> <a href="/wiki/PSoC" class="mw-redirect" title="PSoC">PSoC</a>'s</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Silicon_Laboratories" class="mw-redirect" title="Silicon Laboratories">Silicon Laboratories</a> MCUs</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/8051" class="mw-redirect" title="8051">8051</a> MCUs</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Z80" class="mw-redirect" title="Z80">Z80</a> for <a href="/wiki/CP/M" title="CP/M">CP/M</a><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> and Z80 cross compiler.</li></ul><h3 class="in-block"><span class="mw-headline" id="Silicon_Storage_Technology">Silicon Storage Technology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Microchip_Technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Silicon Storage Technology" data-section="7" class="mw-ui-icon mw-ui-icon-element mw-ui-icon-minerva-edit-enabled edit-page mw-ui-icon-flush-right">Edit</a></span></h3>

<p><b>Silicon Storage Technology, Inc.</b> (SST) was a <a href="/wiki/Sunnyvale,_California" title="Sunnyvale, California">Sunnyvale</a>, <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, United States, technology company producing <a href="/wiki/Non-volatile_memory" title="Non-volatile memory">non-volatile memory</a> devices and related products.<sup id="cite_ref-TID_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TID-24">[24]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-SST2009Q2_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SST2009Q2-25">[25]</a></sup>
SST supplied NOR flash and other <a href="/wiki/Integrated_circuit" title="Integrated circuit">integrated circuits</a> for high-volume applications.<sup id="cite_ref-Profile_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Profile-26">[26]</a></sup></p><p><a href="/wiki/Bing_Yeh" title="Bing Yeh">Bing Yeh</a> co-founded SST in August 1989, and served as its chief executive.<sup id="cite_ref-SVIE_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SVIE-27">[27]</a></sup></p><p>At the 1992 Fall <a href="/wiki/COMDEX" title="COMDEX">COMDEX</a> trade show, SST introduced the first single-board 30 <a href="/wiki/Megabyte" title="Megabyte">MB</a> 2.5" <a href="/wiki/Solid-state_drive" title="Solid-state drive">solid-state drive</a> with standard hard-disk <a href="/wiki/AT_Attachment" class="mw-redirect" title="AT Attachment">ATA</a> interface and a 5 MB <a href="/wiki/PC_Card" title="PC Card">PC Card</a> memory card with built-in controller and firmware.<sup id="cite_ref-Pioneers_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pioneers-28">[28]</a></sup></p><p>In 1993, SST moved its headquarters to <a href="/wiki/Sunnyvale,_California" title="Sunnyvale, California">Sunnyvale</a>. That same year, SST introduced its first SuperFlash technology products, with lower costs and faster write speeds. By the end of 1995, more than 90% of the <a href="/wiki/PC_motherboard" class="mw-redirect" title="PC motherboard">PC motherboards</a> produced in Taiwan had adopted SST's 1 <a href="/wiki/Megabit" title="Megabit">Mbit</a> SuperFlash EEPROM product for the <a href="/wiki/BIOS" title="BIOS">BIOS</a> storage.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> 
The company had its <a href="/wiki/Initial_public_offering" title="Initial public offering">initial public offering</a> November 21, 1995, trading on the <a href="/wiki/NASDAQ" class="mw-redirect" title="NASDAQ">NASDAQ</a> market under the symbol SSTI.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
Analytical models of SuperFlash were published.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>
A five-year licensing agreement was announced in January 1999 with <a href="/wiki/Acer_Inc." title="Acer Inc.">Acer Inc.</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>
A 1997 lawsuit filed by Intel was settled in May 1999 after mediation.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p><p>In 2004, SST began to diversify beyond flash memory products, targeting consumer and industrial products with embedded solid-state data storage and RF wireless communication.<sup id="cite_ref-report04_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-report04-34">[34]</a></sup>
In September 2004 SST purchased a majority stake in Emosyn, which designed products for <a href="/wiki/SIM_card" title="SIM card">SIM cards</a>. In October it announced the acquisition of G-Plus, based in <a href="/wiki/Santa_Monica,_California" title="Santa Monica, California">Santa Monica, California</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-report04_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-report04-34">[34]</a></sup></p><p>In 2006, SST announced a joint development agreement with <a href="/wiki/Taiwan_Semiconductor_Manufacturing_Company" class="mw-redirect" title="Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company">Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company</a> (TSMC) to develop 90 nm SuperFlash technology.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p><p>SST had its stock option grant practices investigated by the US <a href="/wiki/Securities_and_Exchange_Commission" class="mw-redirect" title="Securities and Exchange Commission">Securities and Exchange Commission</a>, ending in June 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup>
It determined it needed to restate earnings, and was given a de-listing notice by NASDAQ for filing late reports from 2006 through 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup>
Business slowed in the <a href="/wiki/Great_Recession" title="Great Recession">Great Recession</a>. The company announced a loss on reduced revenues, reducing its workforce by 17% in December 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p><p>In November 2009, Technology Resource Holdings offered to acquire the company for about $200 million, but a group of shareholders thought it was undervalued.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup>
Starting in February 2010, private equity firm <a href="/wiki/Cerberus_Capital_Management" title="Cerberus Capital Management">Cerberus Capital Management</a> and public company Microchip Technology both made offers to acquire SST.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup>
In April 2010, Microchip completed the acquisition for about $292 million.<sup id="cite_ref-Acquisition_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Acquisition-42">[42]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup>
Microchip sold several SST flash memory assets to <a href="/wiki/Greenliant_Systems" title="Greenliant Systems">Greenliant Systems</a> (founded by Yeh) in May of that year.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>
